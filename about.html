Prelude

In 2014, Lawrence Evalyn posted an article on The Toast called "The Gothic Novel 1790-1830: Index of Motifs", which highlighted some of the more outlandish motifs that recurred in Gothic novels during the Romantic Era. The source for this article was Ann B. Tracy's 1981 reference work, The Gothic Novel, 1790-1830: Plot Summaries and Index to Motifs. Years later, when I started graduate school at UCLA, I found a copy of this book in the library and decided to use the motif index to practice building a relational database (titles in one table, motifs in another, etc.) I was interested in seeing what the most popular motifs were and which books featured the weirdest plot elements.

Project

Miriam Posner's Digital Humanities course gave me the perfect opportunity to explore what this data actually looked like, through different visualization techniques. I learned to clean my dataset with OpenRefine and manipulate it with Tableau to see what kinds of patterns emerged. (I attempted to look at at my dataset with Cytoscape and Gephi, but network analysis will have to wait.

However, I also went beyond my original dataset - using the digital versions of the texts to see if there were patterns in language usage or titles, and mapping the approximate locations of London publishers.. I'm not sure if my findings are particularly useful, but they were fun to make.
