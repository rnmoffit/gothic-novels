Literary trends are nothing new. We can recall the spate of "wizard school" novels emerging in the late 1990s, the flood of paranormal romances in the mid-2000s, and the psychological thrillers with "Girl" in the title that have finally seem to have died down. (The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo, Gone Girl, The Girl on the Train -- you get it.

In the late 18th century, the hottest literary trend was the "Gothic novel." Somewhat related to the "sentimental novel" and the "romantic novel," it distinguished itself by including supernatural elements, callbacks to historical times, and high levels of emotion. In fact, one anonymous writer wrote a contemporary recipe for such a novel:

"A recipe for a Gothic novel"
  • Take--An old castle, half of it ruinous.
  • A long gallery, with a great many doors, some secret ones.
  • Three murdered bodies, quite fresh.
  • As many skeletons, in chests and presses.
  • As old woman hanging by the neck; with her throat cut.
  • Assassins and desperadoes 'quant suff.'
  • Noise, whispers, and groans, threescore at least.
  • Mix them together, in the form of three volumes to be taken at any of the watering places, before going to bed.
Anonymous, "Terrorist Novel Writing" in Spirit of the Public Journals for 1797.

In The Gothic Novel, Ann B Tracy summarizes 208 novels and chapbooks (abridged versions bordering on plagiarism) within a specific time frame, and assembled an index of motifs that are shared. Using her work as a dataset, this project explores what kinds of data visualizations can emerge from literary works.

There are some limitations however. This is one person's interpretation of the genre - other people might have used different categories, or chosen to include or exclude certain works. (Tracy writes that she made no distinction between "Gothic variations" or novels that bordered on "sentimentalism".) I have chosen to use the motif index as written rather than group motifs into larger categories.

Amongst the 208 novels, there are four that have permanent places in the English Gothic canon that shaped the genre for years to come. We can compare them to the general works to see how well they fit among the general corpus, or how unique they are in comparison.

**The English novel as we know it emerged in the 18th century. Human nature being what it is, it didn't take long for it to devolve into something we might consider "trashy" - the "sensational" novel, or the "romantic" novel. Add elements of the supernatural and the psychological, and what we know as the "gothic" novel was born.

**The dataset has 208 novels and chapbooks (abridged versions/shortened rip-offs of popular stories). Flipping through the summaries, it becomes apparent that many of these stories "borrow" plot elements, settings, and characters from each other.

Our Key Players

Today, four novels are recognized as having the greatest impact on the genre:

The Castle of Otranto
Horace Walpole

Widely considered to be the "first" gothic novel. Combines medievalism, the supernatural, and drama.

The Mysteries of Udolpho
Ann Radcliffe

Cemented the author's status as a well-paid writer. Focuses on "terror" and the "sublime."

The Monk
Matthew Gregory Lewis

A gruesome and surprisingly graphic story, focusing on "horror" and unexplained magical phenomena.

Frankenstein, or, the Modern Prometheus
Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley

Needs no introduction. Combines early themes with a modern scientific setting.

Jane Austen parodies the genre in Northanger Abbey, in which the main character reads too many gothic novels (all of which are represented in the data set) and believes she is the heroine in the middle of a foreboding adventure. Austen mentions the distain some people have for the genre, but that they are popular for a reason.

Why does this matter?

To modern eyes, these novels are melodramatic, bordering at the ridiculous. Out of all the books in the data set, only Frankenstein has remained popular into the 21st century. But like over-the-top runway looks that rarely make it to stores in their original form, selected elements can be used in other works to great effect. We would not have Wuthering Heights, Dracula, and hundreds of other popular stories without this influx of Gothic tales in the late 18th century.
