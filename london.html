Using the Tracy dataset as-is in a GIS map does not yield a particularly interesting map. We can tell that the majority of books were published in London just by looking at the raw dataset.

On the other hand, by narrowing down our data to only the London publishers, the map looks a little more interesting.

I searched online for scanned copies of first editions since publishers would sometimes include a street address on the title page. I added pins to a Google map to approximate those locations, and then transferred the coordinates over to Tableau so I could include each publisher's number of works.

HOWEVER.

The margin of error here is extremely high. Some title pages listed two streets for a single publisher that were not in the same part of town; in those cases, I chose whichever street was closest to the other points, assuming that some areas had more publishing houses than others. Some areas may not exist anymore: Paternoster Row, a major hub for publishers, was destroyed during WWII.

Locating London has a neat map of historic London that shows what the city looked like in 1746. While I can't plot my own data points on that map, there's plenty of other data filters you can explore on their site.
